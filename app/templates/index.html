NASA Jet Engine Degradation Simulation

The Commercial Modular Aero-Propulsion System Simulation (CMAPSS) dataset was simulated by NASA Ames Research Center to model degradation curves across multiple turbofan engines (Boeing 747/777 twin-spool layouts).

Instead of proprietary and sensitive airline telemetry data, NASA simulated flights by loading turbines at various altitudes, Mach settings, and throttle envelopes, applying progressive mechanical wear factors until failure occurred.

Telemetry Parameters Map:

Operational Settings: Altitudes, Mach numbers, throttle settings, and ambient envelopes.
21 Sensor Channels: Inlet temperatures, fan speeds (Nf/NRc), outlet pressures, BPR bypass ratios, and bleed enthalpies.

RUL Capping Target Strategy

Why Piecewise? A simple linear reduction target assumes the engine degrades at the exact same rate from cycle 1. Our piecewise implementation caps predicted targets at 125 cycles during early stages, modeling actual hardware fatigue curves correctly.

Industrial Maintenance Strategy

Detailing the transitions between reactive, preventive, and predictive models.

1. Reactive Model

"Run-to-failure" paradigm. Wait for an engine breakdown to occur mid-operation. This incurs extreme parts damage costs (₹1.54 Crore+ / $185k+), catastrophic grounding disruptions, delay penances, and safety risks.

2. Preventive Model

Time-based routine servicing. Schedule repairs at fixed operating cycles (e.g. every 100 cycles) regardless of actual engine wear. This replaces healthy turbine components prematurely, wasting up to 35% of their active lifespan.

3. AeroGuard AI Predictive Model

Sensor-guided decision support pipeline. Models real-time sensor deviations to schedule maintenance at the optimal fatigue window. Maximizes parts utilization while completely avoiding unexpected outages.
